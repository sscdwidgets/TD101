01The Origin Story

The day "settlement risk" was born.

It was a sunny June day in 1974 when Herstatt Bank — a German community bank — had thousands of dollar payments queued for New York. Because of the time difference, no NY bank was open before Herstatt closed at 4:30.

By 5 PM, regulators had shut Herstatt down. The dollar leg of every one of those trades simply never arrived.

Fifty years later, tokenized deposits are about to do something the wire system never managed: kill Herstatt risk for good.

02What Is It, Exactly?

A deposit. On a blockchain.

A tokenized deposit is exactly what it sounds like — a regular bank deposit, represented as a token on a distributed ledger.

The dollars still sit on the bank's balance sheet. They're still FDIC-insured. Still subject to the same BSA, sanctions, and consumer-protection rules.

What changes is the recordkeeping — and that changes everything.

06What This Means For Your Bank

Four moves worth thinking about this quarter.

Revisit your digital wallet strategy.

Even if you never issue a token, your customers will hold them — in third-party wallets you don't control. The wallet, not the account, is becoming the home screen for money. The institution that owns the wallet owns the primary relationship; everyone else is just a back-end utility.

Plan for parallel ledgers.

Traditional cores weren't built for 24/7/365 tokenized assets. Most banks will end up running synchronized ledgers — a programmable rail alongside the legacy core — for years. Start scoping integration partners, reconciliation patterns, and the operational model now. This is a multi-year migration, not a one-quarter project.

Think about token-as-collateral.

As Treasuries, money-market funds, and eventually real estate tokenize, customers will hold those assets in wallets rather than custody accounts. You'll want both the ability to lend against tokenized holdings and the operational rails to accept tokens as collateral on existing loans. The institutions that solve token collateral first will unlock a new lending margin.

Don't wait for the perfect playbook.

The rulebook is being written in real time across the FDIC, OCC, Fed, and state regulators. Banks that engage early — through trade-association cohorts, comment letters, and pilot programs — get to shape the interoperability standards, liquidity mechanisms, and economics of issuance. Late movers inherit whatever the early ones built.
